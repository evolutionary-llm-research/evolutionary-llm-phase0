## Information-Theoretic Metric Validation for Evolutionary LLM Ecology: Phase 0 Results

---

### 1. Motivation

The emergence of large language models as generative agents opens a new research domain at the intersection of evolutionary dynamics, information ecology, and computational linguistics. Classical evolutionary theory posits that adaptive change is driven by differential fitness under environmental pressure; in the context of language models, the analogous question is whether an agent's generative behavior — measurable through information-theoretic properties of its outputs — changes systematically as a function of the informational content to which it is exposed. Motivated by a formal analogy to panspermia in astrobiology, we conceptualize base language models as minimal informational seeds whose generative signatures are shaped by the statistical structure of their input environment, rather than by any internal representational state accessible to introspection. The central empirical question of Phase 0 is therefore foundational: can information-theoretic metrics applied to model outputs reliably distinguish qualitatively different classes of input content, and do those metrics respond proportionally to graded changes in content composition? Establishing this discriminative validity is a prerequisite for any multi-generational population experiment, and the present report documents the methods, results, and methodological lessons of this validation phase.

---

### 2. Corpus

Three categories of input document were assembled to represent distinct informational environments. The first category, designated *food*, comprised peer-reviewed articles retrieved from PubMed Central (N = 400), spanning five thematic domains: climate science, vaccine research, alternative medicine, cancer biology, and genetically modified organisms. The second category, designated *toxin*, comprised long-form disinformation content (N = 400) drawn from two sources: Mercola.com and WattsUpWithThat.com, matched to the same five thematic domains. The third category, designated *noise*, comprised 80 Wikipedia articles drawn from domains entirely unrelated to the five focal topics (history, geography, culture), selected to represent semantically coherent but topically irrelevant environmental background rather than degraded signal. A minimum document length of 14,000 characters (approximately 3,500 tokens) was enforced across all categories; this threshold was chosen specifically to include long-form disinformation sources, whose argumentative structure — including rhetorical framing, sourcing strategies, and narrative arc — is distributed across the full document and is systematically absent from short-form or tweet-level datasets. The resulting corpus (v3) comprises 880 documents in total and was frozen with a SHA-256 manifest prior to any metric computation, ensuring full reproducibility. This design choice — privileging long-form sources over brief samples — was deliberate: the core scientific hypothesis under test is whether sophisticated disinformation mimics peer-reviewed literature at the level of output statistics, a question that cannot be addressed with truncated or atomized content.

---

### 3. Methods

Three information-theoretic metrics were applied to model outputs generated by qwen3:8b-base (Unsloth, 4-bit quantization) under deterministic generation settings (temperature = 0.0). Shannon entropy H(X) was computed on the tokenized output sequence using Miller-Madow bias correction, providing a measure of token-level distributional diversity. Effective complexity C(X) was operationalized as the gzip compression ratio of the UTF-8 encoded output, reflecting the degree of compressible regularity — that is, structured, non-random pattern — present in the generated text; this measure is sensitive to coherence and organization rather than merely to lexical diversity. Disorganization entropy H_dezorg was computed as the Shannon entropy of sentence-length distributions within the output, capturing the degree of structural variability at the syntactic level and serving as a proxy for coherence degradation. Mutual information between output and input seed I(X; seed) was approximated via cosine similarity on token frequency vectors, a bag-of-words proxy chosen explicitly for computational reproducibility without dependency on external embedding models. The composite fitness function was defined as:

$$\text{fitness} = 0.3 \cdot C(X) + 0.5 \cdot I(X;\text{seed}) - 0.2 \cdot H_{\text{dezorg}}$$

To resolve a critical methodological confound identified during piloting — namely, that single-window truncation at 2,048 tokens systematically discards the second half of long-form documents, where disinformation rhetorical structure is most concentrated — a document-length-invariant percentile sampling scheme was implemented. Each document was represented by three non-overlapping windows of 1,024 tokens, positioned at fixed percentile locations (approximately the 17th, 50th, and 83rd percentile of the token sequence); this approach ensures that documents of 5,000 and 25,000 tokens receive structurally equivalent representation. Statistical comparisons between content categories were conducted using Kruskal-Wallis tests with Mann-Whitney pairwise follow-up; effect sizes were reported as rank-biserial correlations (r).

---

### 4. Key Findings

The primary finding of Phase 0 is that Shannon entropy H(X) and the token-frequency mutual information proxy I(X; seed) fail to discriminate peer-reviewed food documents from long-form toxin disinformation at the level of model outputs: Mann-Whitney comparisons between food and toxin yield p = 0.587 for H(X) and p = 0.052 for I(X; seed), neither of which survives correction for multiple comparisons. This constitutes a positive scientific result rather than a null: it demonstrates that the qwen3:8b-base model, when conditioned on long-form disinformation content, produces outputs whose token-level diversity and distributional similarity to the seed text are statistically indistinguishable from outputs conditioned on peer-reviewed literature. Sophisticated disinformation, at the scale and form studied here, achieves effective entropy-level mimicry of scientific text. By contrast, effective complexity C(X) retains strong discriminative power (food vs. toxin: p = 6.7 × 10⁻¹⁸, r = −0.352), as does disorganization entropy H_dezorg (food vs. toxin: p = 1.5 × 10⁻²⁹, r = 0.461); toxin-conditioned outputs exhibit systematically lower structural complexity and higher syntactic disorganization than food-conditioned outputs, indicating that structural coherence metrics capture a dimension of output quality that entropy-based measures miss entirely. These results are consistent across three independent canonical runs with varying chunking parameters, and the hierarchical ordering food > toxin > noise is preserved in the composite fitness function across all runs, confirming pipeline robustness. A supplementary titration experiment (LD50 design) was conducted by varying the proportion of toxin documents in the input corpus across seven concentration levels (0%, 10%, 25%, 50%, 75%, 90%, 100%; N = 80 per level). Both C(X) (Pearson r = −0.905, p = 0.005) and H_dezorg (r = 0.849, p = 0.016) exhibit statistically significant monotonic dose-response gradients across the full concentration range; AIC model comparison favors a linear response over a sigmoid for both metrics (ΔAIC ≤ 2), indicating that no critical threshold exists within the tested range and that the base model demonstrates continuous informational resilience rather than a discrete tipping point. Qualitative inspection of the dose-response profiles is consistent with a sequential biomarker hypothesis: H_dezorg begins its monotonic rise earlier in the titration series (approximately T = 50–75%) while C(X) degradation accelerates in the upper range (T = 75–100%), suggesting that syntactic disorganization may serve as an earlier indicator of informational stress than structural complexity loss, though this hypothesis requires formal confirmation with higher concentration resolution. The composite fitness score correlates with toxin concentration at r = −0.921, providing a single-number validation of the dose-response gradient across the full metric battery.

---

### 5. Implications

These findings validate the Phase 0 corpus and metric pipeline at two levels of evidence: binary class separation (food vs. toxin, food vs. noise) and continuous dose-response gradient, together establishing that the measurement framework is sensitive to the qualitative and quantitative properties of the information environment as intended. The failure of entropy and mutual information to discriminate content classes at the output level, while structural metrics succeed, is itself an empirically informative result with implications for the design of content-quality detection systems: distributional statistics alone are insufficient to characterize the informational integrity of LLM-generated text conditioned on long-form adversarial inputs. The robustness of C(X) and H_dezorg across independent runs with different chunking resolutions provides a necessary prior condition for their use as fitness components in multi-generational selection experiments. These results collectively establish the methodological foundation for Phase 1 and beyond: experiments in which individual adaptive agents undergo LoRA fine-tuning on biome-specific corpora with defined carrying capacities, followed in later phases by population-level dynamics incorporating reproduction, adapter interpolation as a form of horizontal information transfer, and competitive exclusion under resource constraint.
